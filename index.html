Literature

Murakami Haruki (村上春樹)

Novels

Hear the Wind Sing

Rat Series: Book 1

1979 | 🐱 7.0/10

Synopsis: A college student on summer break returns to his hometown and reconnects with his old friend (the titular "Rat") at the local bar. This student will reappear as the protagonist and narrator throughout the rest of the Rat series.

Thoughts: This novel doesn't seem to get a lot of love, even from Murakami fanatics, but I'm actually quite fond of it. The atmosphere is classic Murakami, and although Murakami himself may think differently, I don't see much of a gap between this work and his later works.

Pinball, 1973

Rat Series: Book 2

1980 | 🐱 8.8/10

Synopsis: Set a few years after the events of Hear the Wind Sing, our protagonist develops an obsession with the game of pinball and the Rat starts seeing a woman.

Thoughts: Again, this seems to be an uncommon opinion, but I think this is really, really good. At times, this reads like Murakami at his best. This is the first work where you can observe Murakami start to develop the common themes at the core of all his works. An analysis of the meaning of pinball in this novella can be found here.

A Wild Sheep Chase

Rat Series: Book 3

1982 | 🐱 8.0/10

Synopsis: Murakami's first extended novel. Now in his 30s, our protagonist, accompanied by his girlfriend, explores the wintry landscapes of Hokkaido in pursuit of the traces of the Rat and a mysterious sheep with a star-shaped mark.

Thoughts: I really enjoyed the first half of this novel. It's about as Murakami as you can get, filled with lengthy exposition about subjects such as the history of Japanese sheep and the Ainu people with just enough interspersed plot development to keep readers engaged in the larger story. The second half of the novel is much more plot-focused and "engaging", but I didn't really find myself caring too much about the final fate of the Rat. Perhaps I just need to give this one a re-read.

Hard-Boiled Wonderland and the End of the World

1985 | 🐱 10/10

Synopsis: A middle-aged man is caught up in an information war between secretive opposing factions. In a parallel narrative, a newcomer to a mysterious town known as "The End of the World" seeks to uncover the truth behind the town's existence.

Thoughts: This could be Murakami's all-time best work. It doesn't have the emotional power of Norwegian Wood or the literary polish of The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle, but the beauty of the "The End of the World" sequences in light of the revelation at the end of the novel is truly something special. This quality of this novel is made even more evident upon re-reading, with understanding of the true nature of "The End of the World", but nothing can quite replace the feeling of the "eureka" moment experienced during one's initial reading when this true nature is revealed. This is Murakami's most science-fiction-adjacent novel, with detailed explorations of fields such as acoustics, cryptology, and psychology. Coming in with a background in both the natural and formal sciences, reading this novel also made me realize one thing about Murakami: this is one smart man (although he may claim otherwise).

Norwegian Wood

1987 | 🐱 10/10

Synopsis: Toru, a college student, develops a bond with the longtime girlfriend (Naoko) of Toru's deceased best friend over their shared mourning of his death by suicide. As Naoko's mental health deteriorates, an outgoing female classmate, Midori, enters Toru's life.

Thoughts: Opinions on this novel are extremely polarized. Most people will either completely adore it or utterly despise it. I tend to side with the former opinion, although I do agree that this novel is not for everyone. Personally, I think I read this at the absolutely perfect time in my life, where I was both new to Murakami (this was my first Murakami novel) and in an emotionally vulnerable state. Thus, the overly sentimental nature of the novel, highlighted by Murakami's signature beautiful writing, was really able to speak to me and impart a lasting impression on me. In some ways, Norwegian Wood was able to fundamentally change who I was as a person or at least set the stage for those changes to develop. For all these reasons, I kind of don't want to ever re-read this novel. But at the same time, I trust my ability to compartmentalize and preserve the memory of what this novel meant to me during my initial reading. Briefly, I think that those who dislike this novel have a misunderstanding of its core themes. To me, Norwegian Wood is a novel about the essence of life and death. This novel's detractors may be too hung up on its admittedly dissatisfying romantic storylines.
